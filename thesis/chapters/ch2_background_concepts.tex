This chapter establishes the theoretical and practical foundations
required to understand the design and implementation of the
\texttt{MATCH\_RECOGNIZE} engine for Pandas DataFrames. \section{Pattern Matching in Sequential Data Analysis}
\label{sec:bg-pattern-matching}
% ----------------------------------------------------------------

Sequential pattern detection represents a fundamental challenge in
data analysis, where the objective is to identify ordered sequences
of events or states within large datasets.  In contrast to
conventional queries that match rows individually against one or
more predicates, pattern matching takes preceding and subsequent
rows into consideration and returns groups of rows when they follow
a specified pattern.  This makes it possible to retrieve specific
shapes---such as V-, W-, or U-shapes---within the data.  Pattern
matching is therefore well suited to event-driven processes such as
security monitoring, stock-price analysis, sensor-data analysis,
fraud detection, and complex event processing~\cite{OracleMR}.

Unlike traditional relational operations that focus on individual
rows or aggregated values, pattern matching examines the temporal
or logical ordering of data points to extract meaningful sequences.
This capability is essential across many domains including financial
market analysis, fraud detection, IoT sensor monitoring, and
behavioral analytics.

The important distinction is that the ``symbols'' in row-pattern
recognition are not characters in a string.  They are data rows that
become associated with pattern variables such as \texttt{A},
\texttt{B}, or \texttt{DOWN} when their predicates hold.  A row
sequence can therefore be interpreted as a sequence of variable
assignments, which explains why regular-expression-like syntax and
finite automata are natural tools for this problem.  This connection
is used later in the thesis when patterns are tokenized, compiled
into NFAs, and executed over ordered Pandas partitions.


In the past, relational database systems were very good at set-based tasks like selection, projection, joins, and aggregation. However, they did not offer strong tools for sequence analysis. Analysts who needed to detect patterns often had to export data to stream-processing systems, write procedural logic with cursors and temporary tables, or handle processing in the application layer, which could slow things down. Because of these challenges, there was a gap between the amount of sequential data available and the tools to analyze it. With SQL:2016, row pattern recognition was introduced, allowing sequence analysis to be done directly within SQL queries~\cite{ISO2016SQL}.

\subsection{Real-World Applications}
\label{subsec:bg-applications}
% ................................

\paragraph{Financial Market Analysis.}
Detecting V-shaped price patterns---price decline followed by recovery---requires examining ordered sequences of price movements. For example, identifying stocks whose price troughs are successively higher, a ``higher-low'' pattern, signals a potential upward trend. The canonical example used throughout this thesis is the detection of such V-patterns in customer order data.
~\cite{OracleMR}.

\paragraph{Fraud Detection.}
Credit card fraud can show up as certain patterns, like several small test charges before a big purchase, or transactions happening in places that are too far apart to be realistic. Writing these rules in SQL makes them easier to manage than using procedural code.~\cite{petkovic2022fraud}.

\paragraph{IoT and Sensor Data.}
Manufacturing systems monitor sensor readings to detect patterns of equipment degradation, such as a gradual temperature rise followed by a sudden drop, which may indicate imminent failure. Pattern queries over time-series sensor streams are a natural fit for \texttt{MATCH\_RECOGNIZE}~\cite{OracleMR}.

\paragraph{Clickstream and Funnel Analysis.}
E-commerce platforms study how users move through their sites to find out which paths lead to purchases or when people leave without buying. For example, a typical funnel query looks for the sequence ``view~$\rightarrow$ add-to-cart~$\rightarrow$ remove~$\rightarrow$ exit,'' which can show when a customer is unsure about buying. Using \texttt{MATCH\_RECOGNIZE}, analysts can count how many times an item was added to the cart before it was removed, pull the exact timestamps for each action, and measure the total time spent, all in one query. Without this feature, these tasks would need several complex subqueries or extra session logic. These types of funnel analyses are common uses of row-pattern recognition in analytical SQL~\cite{hoffa2021snowflake}.

\paragraph{Network Security and Intrusion Detection.}
Cyber-attacks usually do not appear as just one unusual event. Instead, they happen through a series of steps. For example, a multi-stage intrusion might start with port scanning, then move to repeated failed logins, followed by a successful login, and finally privilege escalation. With \texttt{MATCH\_RECOGNIZE}, this whole sequence can be described in a single pattern query over the event log. The query flags the suspicious session and pulls out the first and last timestamps, the targeted port, and the compromised account, all without exporting the log to another SIEM tool~\cite{petkovic2022fraud}.

\paragraph{Healthcare and Patient Monitoring.}
Early warning systems in clinical data streams help spot signs of physiological decline before a crisis happens. For example, sepsis often shows up as a rising heart rate, then a drop in blood pressure, and finally a spike in white blood cell count. This pattern shows how ordered clinical measurements can be modeled as a row-pattern query over a time series of vital signs. The specific alerting process, however, depends on the domain and system in use.


\paragraph{Supply Chain and Logistics.}
Supply chain disruptions can quickly set off a chain reaction. For instance, a single late shipment might lead to several partial deliveries and eventually a stockout. Applying \texttt{MATCH\_RECOGNIZE} to an order fulfillment log uncovers these escalation patterns for each supplier or warehouse and shows how much time passes from the first delay to the final stockout. Teams can then look into issues earlier, instead of waiting until after problems have already happened.


\paragraph{Financial Compliance and Trade Surveillance.}
Trade-surveillance systems watch for signs of market manipulation, like ``layering.'' In this scheme, a trader places a large resting order to influence the price, then trades on the other side, and cancels the first order. This three-step process, with its timing, can be described using a \texttt{MATCH\_RECOGNIZE} pattern, which makes a complex compliance rule into an auditable SQL query.

\section{The SQL:2016 \texttt{MATCH\_RECOGNIZE} Clause}
\label{sec:bg-match-recognize}
% ----------------------------------------------------------------

The SQL:2016 standard introduced \texttt{MATCH\_RECOGNIZE}~\cite{ISO2016SQL,iso2016}, which brings pattern matching across rows into SQL. The feature finds ordered sequences of rows where each part of the pattern is defined by a row condition, not just a character, and allows complex event sequences to be described in a clear, declarative way~\cite{winand2017sqlnew}. While it does not add new capabilities to the relational model since similar queries can be written with joins, window functions, recursion, or procedural code, it offers a simpler and more maintainable way to express sequence-based logic.
  

At the time of writing, PostgreSQL~18 lists the SQL row-pattern features
as unsupported, while DuckDB lists \texttt{MATCH\_RECOGNIZE} as planned
work~\cite{postgresql18unsupported,duckdb2026roadmap,howe2020matchrecognize,findling2025bringing}.  This thesis aims
to make declarative row-pattern matching possible in Pandas DataFrames
and in-process Python analytics.

The SQL standard distinguishes three optional features for row pattern
recognition~\cite{winand_match_recognize}.  \textbf{Feature~R010}
covers row pattern recognition in the \texttt{FROM} clause.
\textbf{Feature~R020} covers row patterns in \texttt{WINDOW}
specifications.  \textbf{Feature~R030} covers full aggregate support.
They are distinct parts of the standard, not successive
levels~\cite{ISO2021,michels2018new,melton2017sqlrpr}.

This thesis uses the SQL:2016 terminology as the conceptual frame,
but the implementation claims are intentionally scoped.  The proposed
engine targets core R010-style behavior in the \texttt{FROM} clause
and evaluates selected row-pattern constructs that are relevant to
in-memory Pandas analytics.  Features outside this scope, including
full window-clause row-pattern recognition and complete vendor-specific
extensions, are treated as related work or future work rather than as
requirements of the implemented system.

\subsection{Clause Structure and Components}
\label{subsec:bg-clause-structure}
% ................................
This clause is used with a rowset, which means it can work with a table or a derived table. It is typically described using eight related sub-clauses. Some of these are necessary, while others are optional Listing~\ref{lst:mr-syntax}. \texttt{PATTERN} and \texttt{DEFINE} are the main required clauses for row pattern recognition. The other clauses control how data is split, sorted, shown, and how overlaps are managed.

 

\begin{lstlisting}[
    language     = SQL,
    caption      = {General syntax of the \texttt{MATCH\_RECOGNIZE} clause},
    label        = {lst:mr-syntax}
]
SELECT <columns>
FROM   <table>
MATCH_RECOGNIZE (
    [PARTITION BY <partition_columns>] -- 1. Data organization
    [ORDER BY     <ordering_columns>]  -- 2. Row sequencing
    [MEASURES     <expressions>]       -- 3. Output computations
    [<rows_per_match>]                 -- 4. Result granularity
    [AFTER MATCH SKIP <option>]        -- 5. Overlap handling
    PATTERN ( <pattern_regex> )        -- 6. Sequence specification
    [SUBSET <union_variable_defs>]     -- 7. Union variables
    DEFINE <variable_definitions>      -- 8. Variable predicates
) AS <alias>
\end{lstlisting}

\begin{enumerate}

\item \textbf{PARTITION BY.}
If provided, this option splits the input rowset into separate partitions, similar to how window functions work. Pattern matching then runs on each partition by itself, which allows for parallel processing and grouping by attributes such as customer or sensor. If the clause is omitted, the input is handled as one partition.

\item \textbf{ORDER BY.}
This determines how rows are ordered within each partition. When an order is set, the pattern-matching algorithm processes the rows in that sequence. Consistent results usually require an explicit order. Several sort keys may be combined, each with its own \texttt{ASC} or \texttt{DESC} direction.


\item \textbf{MEASURES.}
  Optionally defines computed expressions that extract values from
  matched row sequences using pattern variables and navigation functions
  (\texttt{FIRST}, \texttt{LAST}, \texttt{PREV}, \texttt{NEXT}).
  Each measure expression becomes a column in the output, allowing a
  match to be summarized without returning every raw input row.

\item \textbf{Output options.}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item \texttt{ONE ROW PER MATCH}: one summary row per complete
      match.
    \item \texttt{ALL ROWS PER MATCH}: one row for every row
      participating in a match.
    \item \texttt{WITH UNMATCHED ROWS}: an
      \texttt{ALL ROWS PER MATCH} modifier that includes
      non-matching rows in the result where supported.
  \end{itemize}

\item \textbf{AFTER MATCH SKIP.}
  Controls how the engine resumes after finding a match:
  \begin{itemize}
    \item \texttt{SKIP TO NEXT ROW} -- resume from the row after
      match start (maximum overlaps).
    \item \texttt{SKIP PAST LAST ROW} -- resume from the row after
      match end (non-overlapping matches).
    \item \texttt{SKIP TO FIRST} \textit{var} -- resume from the
      first row classified as \textit{var}.
    \item \texttt{SKIP TO LAST} \textit{var} -- resume from the last
      row classified as \textit{var}.
  \end{itemize}

\item \textbf{PATTERN.}
  Specifies the sequence using regular-expression-like syntax with
  pattern variables as terminals:
  \begin{itemize}
    \item Concatenation: \texttt{A~B~C} (A followed by B followed by C).
    \item Alternation: \texttt{A~|~B} (either A or B).
    \item Quantifiers: \texttt{A*} (zero or more), \texttt{A+}
      (one or more), \texttt{A?} (zero or one), \texttt{A\{n\}}
      (exactly $n$), \texttt{A\{n,m\}} (between $n$ and $m$).
    \item Grouping: \texttt{(A~B)+} (one or more AB pairs).
    \item Anchors: \texttt{\^{}A~B~C\$} (pattern must span the
      entire partition).
    \item Exclusions: \texttt{\{-~A+~B+~-\}} (rows matching the
      enclosed sub-pattern are excluded from row-level output).
    \item Permutations: \texttt{PERMUTE(A,~B,~C)} (any ordering
      of A, B, and C).
  \end{itemize}

\item \textbf{SUBSET.}
This optional clause defines union variables, which are made up of variables already listed in the \texttt{PATTERN} clause. A union variable does not change how matching works. Instead, it gives a shared name to rows matched by any of its component variables. A union variable may appear in \texttt{MEASURES}, \texttt{DEFINE}, and skip expressions where allowed.


\item \textbf{DEFINE.}
Boolean predicates are assigned to pattern variables. When matching, candidate rows are checked against these predicates in the current match context to see if they can be classified as a specific variable. Predicates can use current-row columns, navigation functions, aggregations over matched subsequences, and Boolean or comparison operators~\cite{winand_match_recognize}. If a pattern variable does not have a \texttt{DEFINE} predicate, it is treated as an unconstrained variable.
 

\end{enumerate}

A \texttt{MATCH\_RECOGNIZE} clause is embedded in a
\texttt{SELECT} query and can therefore be further processed by
additional specifications.  Possible extensions include applying
groupings or aggregate functions to the outer
\texttt{SELECT}~\cite{winand_match_recognize}.

These sub-clauses make a clear distinction between the pattern to detect and the way the engine searches for it. The \texttt{PATTERN} clause sets the sequence's structure, the \texttt{DEFINE} clause sets the rules for labeling rows, and the output and skip clauses decide how matches are shown. This separation is key to how the system is built in later chapters. First, parsing creates a structured version of the query. Then, validation checks that the variables and columns used are correct. Finally, the pattern part is turned into an automaton for execution.

\subsection{Illustrative Example: Stock Price V-Pattern}
\label{subsec:bg-example}
% ................................

Listing~\ref{lst:mr-example} shows a \texttt{MATCH\_RECOGNIZE}
query that detects V-shaped price patterns in customer order data.

\begin{lstlisting}[
    language = SQL,
    caption  = {Detecting V-shaped price patterns with \texttt{MATCH\_RECOGNIZE}},
    label    = {lst:mr-example}
]
SELECT customer_id,
       start_price, bottom_price, final_price,
       start_date,  final_date
FROM   orders
MATCH_RECOGNIZE (
    PARTITION BY customer_id
    ORDER BY     order_date
    MEASURES
        START.price          AS start_price,
        LAST(DOWN.price)     AS bottom_price,
        LAST(UP.price)       AS final_price,
        START.order_date     AS start_date,
        LAST(UP.order_date)  AS final_date
    ONE ROW PER MATCH
    AFTER MATCH SKIP PAST LAST ROW
    PATTERN (START DOWN+ UP+)
    DEFINE
        DOWN AS price < PREV(price),
        UP   AS price > PREV(price)
);
\end{lstlisting}


The query groups rows by \texttt{customer\_id} and sorts them by \texttt{order\_date}. It looks for patterns that start with any row, followed by one or more rows where the price drops each time, and then one or more rows where the price rises each time. When it finds a match, the \texttt{MEASURES} part pulls out the starting price and date, the lowest price from the last \texttt{DOWN} row, and the final price from the last \texttt{UP} row.

\section{Chapter Summary}
\label{sec:bg-summary}
% ----------------------------------------------------------------
This chapter explained sequential pattern matching as an analytical problem and showed why regular row-by-row predicates do not work well for many tasks involving ordered data. It also covered the main parts of SQL:2016 \texttt{MATCH\_RECOGNIZE}, such as partitioning, ordering, measures, output modes, skip policies, pattern definitions, and variable predicates.

The stock-price V-pattern example demonstrated how these components fit together in a full query and explained why this abstraction works well with automata-based execution. Chapter~\ref{chap:related} looks more closely at existing implementations, comparing their features, deployment options, and real-world limitations.
